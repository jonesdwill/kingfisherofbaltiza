🔧 Final Preparations

Rigging, provisions, and ice gear — getting Kingfisher of Baltiza ready for departure

Preparation 22 May 2025 By Meg Loftus

We are now three weeks from departure and the boatyard has become something of a second home. The to-do list was long in January; it is now short but intense — the kind of list where every remaining item is important and none of them can be rushed.

The Rigging

The standing rigging was the big one. The shrouds and forestay were the originals from the 2009 re-rig — technically still within tolerance, but I wasn't prepared to gamble on them over 3,000 miles of North Atlantic ocean. Everything has been replaced with new 1x19 stainless wire, every toggle and T-terminal inspected, and the furling headstay swapped for a new Selden unit. The running rigging had already been replaced last year, but I took the opportunity to add a second set of reefing lines led back to the cockpit — essential for singlehanded sailing in the kind of conditions we might encounter.

There's a saying among offshore sailors: never go to sea on anything you wouldn't bet your life on. The rigging is exactly that bet.

Provisions

Ninety days of food for two people doesn't sound like much until you try to stow it. We've done two complete provisions inventories and a full stowage exercise, working out how to maintain trim as supplies are consumed. The staples are freeze-dried expedition meals for the offshore passages and canned and dried goods for the Greenland period, supplemented — we hope — by fresh fish.

Water is carried in two 100-litre tanks plus a further 80 litres in jerry cans. The watermaker was serviced last month and performs reliably; in Greenland we'll also be able to fill from melt-water streams ashore. Coffee consumption has been budgeted for at approximately three cups per person per day. Some things are non-negotiable.

Ice Gear

This is the category that makes the Greenland preparations different from any other passage I've done. The ice pole — a reinforced aluminium sounding pole for probing around growlers in poor visibility — has been fitted to the bow rail. Immersion suits for two are stowed in a dedicated bag in the cockpit locker, exactly where they will be needed and always accessible.

The bow has been reinforced with additional internal framing, and a full set of heavy rubber fenders is rigged along the waterline for use when lying against ice. None of this makes Kingfisher an icebreaker. The idea is never to hit ice hard; the preparation is for the times when ice finds you.

What's Left

The engine was serviced two weeks ago and runs sweetly. The radar reflector has been upgraded to a larger active unit. The flares have been restocked. The chart portfolio is complete (both electronic and paper). The satellite communicator has been tested and the shore contact briefed on our check-in schedule.

What remains is time — and time is running out, beautifully, in the way it does when you're nearly ready for the thing you've been planning for years. Three weeks. Scotland to Greenland. I can barely believe it.
