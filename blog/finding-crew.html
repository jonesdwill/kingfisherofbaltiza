🧑‍✈️ Finding Crew for an Arctic Passage

How to find and vet people for one of the most demanding voyages you can make in a small boat

Crew 28 February 2025 By Meg Loftus

Sailing to Greenland alone is theoretically possible. A number of experienced singlehanders have done it. But for a first Arctic passage in a boat I was still learning, I decided early on that I wanted at least one additional crew member — ideally two — for the offshore legs. Finding them turned out to be more interesting than I expected.

What I Was Looking For

The requirements were not primarily about sailing ability, though that mattered. What I was really looking for was psychological compatibility for a long, confined, often uncomfortable passage; genuine willingness to take watch at two in the morning in cold, wet conditions; and the emotional maturity to stay calm when things get difficult — because on a passage like this, things will get difficult.

I also needed someone with good practical skills: engines, electrics, first aid. A person who panics when the navigation system fails is less useful than a person who says "right, let's think about this" and gets the paper charts out.

Where to Find Them

I posted on three crew-finding platforms: findacrew.net, the Ocean Cruising Club's forum, and a Facebook group for high-latitude sailing. The response was substantial — apparently "Greenland" in a passage description acts as a filter that eliminates casual enquiries very effectively. Most of the people who replied were serious offshore sailors.

I also put the word out through my sailing club and the World Cruising Club network. This yielded two of the best conversations I had — people known to people I trust, which is a different quality of referral than an internet advert.

The Vetting Process

Video calls first, to talk through the passage and get a sense of the person. Then, for anyone I was seriously considering, a day sail together on Kingfisher. You can learn an enormous amount about someone by sailing with them for eight hours.

Watch how they handle a problem under pressure. Watch whether they ask good questions or tell you things. Watch whether they're still cheerful after four hours of tacking into a Force 5 in the Firth of Lorne.

I checked first-aid and sea survival certificates as a baseline. I asked for references from previous skippers and followed them up. One person who presented brilliantly in conversation turned out, per a previous skipper, to have been a serious morale problem on a long passage. That reference saved us both considerable discomfort.

The Crew

The crew for the main offshore legs is now confirmed. I won't share details here until we've met in person in Oban before departure, but I can say that I'm genuinely delighted with both of them — competent, enthusiastic, and the kind of people I'd want around in a Force 8 at 70 degrees North.

The Greenland portion itself I expect to do largely alone or with short-term visitors joining in port. That part is about exploration and taking time — the kind of sailing where solo or very short-handed works best. More on that closer to the time.
