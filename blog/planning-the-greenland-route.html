📍 Planning the Greenland Route

Pilot books, ice charts, and the accumulated wisdom of those who went before

Planning 10 April 2025 By Meg Loftus

Planning an offshore passage to a well-charted destination like, say, the Azores is straightforward enough: pilot book, weather router, departure. Planning a passage to the east coast of Greenland is a different proposition entirely. The charts, in places, have not been properly surveyed since the nineteenth century. The ice is unpredictable. The weather is extreme. And there is almost no infrastructure — no marinas, no fuel docks, no rescue services within easy reach.

The Pilot Books

I started with the Admiralty Arctic Pilot (NP11), which covers the general principles of Arctic navigation — ice formation and behaviour, magnetic anomalies at high latitude, weather patterns, and emergency procedures. Essential reading, though its level of detail for specific anchorages is limited.

The single most useful planning resource has been the RCC Pilotage Foundation's Arctic and Northern Waters guide, which brings together cruising reports from dozens of yachts over the past thirty years. The anchorage notes are invaluable. So is the honest assessment of what conditions to expect in the Denmark Strait in July: not good, but manageable.

Ice Charts

The Danish Meteorological Institute publishes weekly ice charts for Greenlandic waters throughout the navigation season. I've been downloading and studying them since January, trying to understand the pattern of how the ice retreats through the summer. The east Greenland coast typically begins to open up in July, though the timing varies considerably from year to year.

Scoresby Sund, our primary destination, usually clears by late July in a good year. The challenge is that "usually" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. I've been in contact with Torsten Kana at the Danish Arctic Institute in Copenhagen, whose ice-route predictions are the best available for the region.

"The ice doesn't read the pilot books," a friend with Greenland experience told me. "You watch it. You wait. You go when it says you can go."

The Route in Detail

Leaving from Oban on the west coast of Scotland, the first leg to the Faroe Islands is about 450 nautical miles — a well-worn passage and a good warm-up for the crew. From the Faroes, we cross to Reykjavík in Iceland: another 500 miles or so, exposed but manageable.

The Iceland–Greenland crossing is the crux: about 800 miles across the Denmark Strait, which has a deserved reputation for very short, steep seas and unpredictable gales. We'll wait in Iceland for a good weather window, potentially for days or even a week or two. There is no point hurrying this decision.

Once into Greenlandic waters, the pilotage becomes local and tactical: following the ice edge, using the inshore leads where the water is clearer, anchoring early when the evening fog rolls in. The destination — Scoresby Sund — is about 70°N, well inside the Arctic Circle.

Permits and Paperwork

Foreign yachts visiting Greenland require advance notification to the Greenlandic authorities. No formal permit is required for most anchorages, but the National Parks on the east coast are subject to additional regulations, and we've obtained the necessary permissions. The paperwork took longer than expected — not because of bureaucratic difficulty, but because every communications thread led to fascinating conversations about the ecology and geology of the region.

Planning this voyage has been, in many ways, as rewarding as the voyage itself will be. More dispatches soon.
