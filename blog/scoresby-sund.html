🧊 Scoresby Sund

The world's largest fjord system — and the reason this voyage began

Greenland 14 January 2025 By Meg Loftus

The destination that set this whole voyage in motion is a place called Scoresby Sund — or Kangertittivaq in Greenlandic — on the east coast of Greenland at approximately 70 degrees North. It is the world's largest fjord system, a branching network of sea channels that penetrates more than 350 kilometres into the Greenlandic interior. On a map, it looks like a river delta in reverse — the sea reaching deep into the land, surrounded by peaks that rise sheer from the water to over two thousand metres.

Scale and Remoteness

The scale is hard to grasp from a chart. The entrance to the fjord system is itself over 30 kilometres wide. Inside, the channels spread and bifurcate; you could spend a month exploring and not see everything. The nearest town — Ittoqqortoormiit (Scoresbysund), the settlement that gives the fjord its name — has a population of around 350 people and is one of the most isolated permanently inhabited places in the world.

There is no road to Ittoqqortoormiit. There is no ferry service. The only ways to reach it are by air (a small charter flight from Iceland in summer), by icebreaker supply ship once or twice a year, or by sailing vessel.

The Wildlife

One of the draws, for me personally as a marine biologist, is the extraordinary concentration of wildlife. The fjord supports large populations of ringed and bearded seals, which in turn attract polar bears. Musk oxen graze on the tundra vegetation above the shoreline. Narwhals are reported in some of the inner arms in summer. Seabirds breed in huge colonies on the clifftops. In the water: Arctic char, Greenland halibut, and — this is the one I'm most hoping for — bowhead whales, one of the few large whale species I have never yet seen.

There are places on this planet where the relationship between the living world and the geological world is still raw and immediate. Scoresby Sund is one of them. You can see glaciers calving icebergs directly into fjords that were first charted by Europeans less than two centuries ago.

The Ice

The Scoresby Sund area is ice-affected for much of the year. The inner fjords may retain sea ice until July; the outer entrance can be blocked by drifting pack ice well into summer in a cold year. The icebergs that fill the fjord in summer are calved from the outlet glaciers of the Greenland Ice Sheet, some of them the size of cathedrals.

This is not background scenery. This is the environment you are sailing through. Everything about the navigation — the watch schedule, the speed, the anchoring choices — is structured around the reality of the ice. It demands a different kind of attention than any other sailing I've done.

Why Now

The answer, in one word, is change. The Greenland Ice Sheet is losing mass faster than at any time in the modern record. The sea ice that has defined the rhythm of life in Scoresby Sund for thousands of years is retreating. The ecology of the fjord — the bears, the seals, the walrus that used to haul out on sea ice — is being profoundly altered.

I want to see it while it still looks like the place I've been reading about for twenty years. And yes, I know that's a complicated thought to sit with. But I'd rather bear witness and talk about it than stay away and pretend it's fine.
