💡 How the Idea Was Born

A tattered book, a winter evening, and the beginning of a five-year plan

The Dream 20 December 2024 By Meg Loftus

The idea came, as the best sailing ideas do, from a book — specifically from a tattered paperback copy of H.W. Tilman's Mischief in Greenland found on the shelves of a second-hand bookshop in Oban on a rainy Tuesday afternoon in November 2019. I was supposed to be buying birthday presents. I bought the Tilman instead, and six others in the series, and spent the rest of November reading about an elderly converted pilot cutter making extraordinary voyages to Baffin Island, Greenland, and the Heard Island, crewed by a rotating cast of volunteers of varying competence.

The Tilman Standard

Tilman sailed to places that were genuinely remote and genuinely difficult, in a boat that was not designed for the task, with crew he recruited by advertisement in The Times. His writing is laconic to the point of comedy — weeks of terrifying conditions dispatched in a paragraph — and his attitude to hardship is one of cheerful acceptance rather than drama. He is exactly the wrong role model for someone planning a serious offshore voyage. He is also exactly the right one.

What Tilman demonstrated, above all, is that the limiting factor in ocean sailing is not the boat or the conditions but the will to go. You could always find a reason not to: the boat isn't ready, the weather window isn't ideal, I need more experience. Tilman's response to all of these objections was essentially "well then, let's go and get some."

"The sea, among other things, is a great teacher of the wisdom of simplicity." — H.W. Tilman

From Idea to Plan

It took five years between the bookshop and departure. There was no particular plan for the first year — just reading, talking to people, and attending a couple of ocean sailing seminars. Then I found Kingfisher of Baltiza, a 1982 steel sloop with good bones and a survey that confirmed she was worth the asking price. I bought her in 2021 and the planning got serious.

The five years were not, in retrospect, time wasted. They were time spent accumulating the kind of knowledge that doesn't come from books: watching how a steel boat behaves in different conditions, building a relationship with an engine that will keep us alive in calms, learning to read weather with the granular attention that the North Atlantic demands. And they were time spent saving enough money that I could do this properly rather than cheaply — which in Arctic sailing is a meaningful distinction.

The Name

People often ask about the boat's name. Kingfisher of Baltiza — where is Baltiza? It's not a place I've been able to find on any chart or map, and the previous owner (now in his eighties and no longer sailing) wasn't sure himself. He thought it might be a place in the Netherlands, near where the boat was built, but he couldn't be certain. Somewhere along the way, the provenance of the name was lost.

I kept the name. It's part of the boat's identity now, and there's something apt about sailing to a place many people haven't heard of on a boat named for a place nobody can find. The kingfisher is also a bird of rivers and coasts — and we are going to some of the most dramatic coasts on Earth.
