🛶 Why a Steel Boat for Ice Waters

The case for steel hulls when sailing in ice-prone high latitudes

The Boat 15 March 2025 By Meg Loftus

When I was looking for a boat to take to Greenland, the hull material question came up in practically every conversation I had with experienced high-latitude sailors. Fibreglass is lighter, easier to maintain, and the dominant material in modern production yachts. But for a voyage to ice-affected waters, steel kept coming up as the preference of those who had actually been there.

The Ice Argument

The primary argument for steel in ice waters is not that you intend to hit things — you emphatically do not — but that when something hits you, steel offers a margin of safety that fibreglass cannot. Growlers (submerged chunks of ice, often the size of a car or larger) can be invisible in any kind of chop. Bergy bits lurk in fog. At night, even with radar, you may not see everything.

A fibreglass hull struck by a growler can be holed catastrophically. A steel hull — if the plate is thick enough and the framing sound — may dent and deform, but it is far more likely to remain watertight. The margin isn't infinite; steel hulls can be holed too. But in a remote location hundreds of miles from the nearest assistance, any margin matters.

Think of it as the difference between a car with and without a crumple zone — but the crumple zone is the hull itself, not a separate safety feature.

The Structural Story

Kingfisher of Baltiza has 6mm steel plating, which is on the heavier end for a 38-footer. The framing is closely spaced. The keel is a ballasted steel fin, integral with the hull — no bolt-on keel to worry about. The rudder stock is 316 stainless passing through a bronze stuffing box in a steel skeg. Everything is over-engineered for load-bearing.

When I surveyed her, the surveyor — a man who has inspected hundreds of steel boats — said the hull was in better condition than most steel boats he sees at half her age. The blasting and re-coating done in 2019 was thorough, and the underwater sections show no active corrosion. Annual inspections will continue throughout the expedition.

The Downsides

Steel is not without disadvantages, and it would be dishonest not to acknowledge them.

Weight: A steel boat of a given length is heavier than her fibreglass equivalent, which affects performance — particularly in light airs. This matters less in the North Atlantic than in the trade winds. We'll motor in calms and sail hard when the wind fills in.

Corrosion: Steel corrodes. Maintaining the coating inside and out is an ongoing commitment. I've added a cathodic protection system (sacrificial anodes and a hull monitoring unit) and expect to carry out annual hull inspections and touch-up painting as a matter of routine.

Compass deviation: A steel hull affects the magnetic compass, requiring careful deviation correction. I've had the compass professionally swung and use GPS as primary reference in any case.

The Verdict

For the specific voyage I'm planning — to the ice-affected waters of east Greenland, single or double-handed, far from any assistance — the case for steel is clear. I chose Kingfisher of Baltiza precisely because she is steel, and I've never regretted it.

Would I take a well-found fibreglass cruising boat to Greenland? Possibly, with a very experienced crew and a conservative ice-avoidance strategy. Would I take one singlehanded? No. Not to those waters. Not me.
