🧊 The Greenland Expedition

A summer voyage from Scotland's west coast to the fiords of East Greenland — through ice, ocean, and some of the most remote waters on Earth.

🧊 East Greenland Fiords

Why Greenland?

Greenland's east coast is one of the most spectacular and least-visited cruising grounds on the planet. Sheer mountains rise thousands of metres from near-freezing fjords crowded with icebergs calved from ancient glaciers. The wildlife — whales, polar bears, musk oxen, arctic foxes — is extraordinary.

For Meg Loftus, skipper of Kingfisher of Baltiza, the attraction is exactly that combination: extreme remoteness, demanding seamanship, and natural beauty on an almost incomprehensible scale.

The 2025 expedition aims to reach the Scoresby Sund area — the world's largest fjord system — before the season closes and the pack ice returns. Success depends on weather windows, ice conditions, and the reliability of a single steel-hulled vessel far from any assistance.

Schedule

Expedition Timeline

A rough outline — the ocean and ice have the final say on exact dates.

Winter 2024–25

Preparation & Fit-Out

Rigging overhaul, new chartplotter with Arctic overlays, ice pole installation, safety gear refresh, crew recruitment, and months of passage planning.

Spring 2025

Sea Trials & Final Shake-Down

Offshore shakedown passages around the Scottish islands to test all systems under load before the main expedition begins.

June 2025

Departure — Oban, Scotland

Slipping lines from Oban, threading through the Hebrides, and making the first offshore passage north-east toward the Faroe Islands.

Late June 2025

Faroe Islands

A short stop in Tórshavn for rest, weather assessment, and final fuel and fresh provisions before the Atlantic crossing to Iceland.

Early July 2025

Iceland — Reykjavík

Arrival in Iceland for a longer pause: engine service, thorough ice-conditions briefing, and a meeting with the Icelandic Coastguard before pressing on west.

Mid July 2025

Greenland — Landfall

Crossing the Denmark Strait and making landfall on the east Greenland coast. Timing depends on ice — this leg cannot be rushed.

July–August 2025

Exploring Scoresby Sund

Spending three to four weeks in the Scoresby Sund fjord system — the world's largest — among icebergs, wildlife, and total wilderness.

Late August 2025

Homeward Bound

Departing Greenland ahead of the autumn storms, returning via Iceland and the Faroe Islands to arrive back in Scotland by September.

September 2025

Homecoming — Scotland

Slipping back into a Scottish harbour — tired, full of stories, and very ready for a warm shower and a dram of whisky.

Preparation

Getting Ready for the Ice

High-latitude sailing demands a different level of preparation from normal offshore passages.

🔧

Boat Modifications

Ice pole for probing growlers, extra reinforcement at the bow, heavy-duty fendering, and redundant navigation systems for when GPS signals are poor at high latitude.

🆘

Safety & Emergency Kit

Full immersion suits for the whole crew, life raft for four, EPIRB, PLBs, a satellite communicator for two-way messaging, and a thorough MOB drill regime.

🗂️

Navigation & Charts

Paper charts for the whole route (electronic is primary, paper is backup), ice-chart subscriptions, and Arctic pilot books cross-referenced with recent cruising reports.

🥫

Provisions

90 days of food for two people, stowed carefully to maintain trim. Freeze-dried and canned goods for the remote legs, fresh food in the ice box when available.

🐻

Wildlife & Bear Awareness

East Greenland has polar bears. Every landing ashore includes a bear-watch protocol. Flare guns and noise-makers are part of the shore-kit.

📡

Communications

Iridium satellite phone for voice and data, a dedicated sat-messenger for position tracking, and daily check-in schedules with a shore contact in the UK.
